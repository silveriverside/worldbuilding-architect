# Sci-Fi Toolkit: grounding the big elements

The user listed a wide arsenal: androids/bioroids, mutants, aliens, hybrids, gene-editing, clones,
mechanical ascension, prosthetics, mind-syncing, brain-in-a-vat, the Matrix/mother-construct, VR,
black holes, fusion, space elevators, space travel, fleets, bounty hunters, bodily immortality,
robots, hyper-advanced alien tech, higher dimensions, wormholes, time travel, timeline-jumping,
arks/underground refuges. This file is about making any of these feel *earned* rather than decorative.

## Three rules for every sci-fi element

1. **Lead with the consequence, not the gadget.** Don't explain how the upload works; show the divorce
   court arguing over which copy keeps the kids. The social, legal, economic, and emotional fallout is
   where the worldbuilding is. Tech is interesting only through what it does to people.
2. **One change ripples outward.** Any capability rewrites adjacent institutions. Cheap fusion guts
   petro-states and the navies that guarded sea lanes; bodily immortality breaks inheritance,
   pensions, and the very idea of "generations"; perfect VR collapses the real-estate logic of cities.
   When you introduce a tech, ask what *three institutions it breaks* and build those.
3. **Keep costs, limits, and failure modes.** Magic that always works is boring and consequence-free.
   Every tech needs a price (energy, time, bodies, sanity, dependence), a limit (what it *can't* do),
   and a characteristic failure (how it goes wrong) — the failures are plot fuel and undercurrents.

## Naming these elements (cross-ref naming-provenance.md)

These are exactly where invented worlds get cheesy. Ground every one:
- Don't call it a "Neural Synchronization Matrix." Call it what the people who used it first called it
  — a brand ("Halos"), a place ("the Pavlov ward"), an eponym ("a Vance"), a worn acronym ("Celis"),
  or slang ("getting shelved"). Log it in the lexicon.
- Alien/higher-dimensional things should resist clean human names — humans approximate, mispronounce,
  or name them after the human who died meeting them. The wrongness becomes canonical.

## Quick consequence-prompts per element

Use these as seeds; pick the few the world actually needs, don't include everything.

- **Mind upload / sync / brain-in-vat:** identity & continuity law; the divergence problem (copies
  drift); who can delete an instance; class divide between the backed-up and the mortal.
- **Prosthetics / cyber-limbs:** firmware ownership & DRM on your own body; black-market mods; a
  recall that maims thousands (a perfect undercurrent); the un-augmented as a despised or sainted
  minority.
- **Clones / gene-editing:** lineage and legitimacy; editing as class marker; the un-edited as
  "naturals"; a banned trait that resurfaces; armies vs. individuals debate.
- **Androids / robots / AI:** personhood thresholds; labor collapse and who eats; an AI that asks for
  a *name* (tie to naming culture); the question of whether obedience is slavery.
- **Aliens / first contact / hyper-advanced tech:** *whose physics is correct?*; gifts that are traps;
  trade in incomprehensible goods; the figure/ground flip (we're the primitive frontier to them).
- **Hybrids / mutants:** the politics of the in-between; who counts as which "we"; passing and outing.
- **Fusion / space elevator / FTL / wormholes / fleets:** what old power they obsolete (and that
  power's revenge); choke points become geopolitics ("the throat"); the first disaster that defines
  the route; spacer vs. planet-dweller culture split.
- **Bodily immortality:** broken inheritance and pensions; gerontocracy; the suicide-as-only-exit
  problem; a generation that never cedes power (undercurrent → revolt).
- **Black holes / higher dimensions / time & timeline travel:** keep these *rare and expensive* and
  treat them as worldview-breakers, not commutes. Time/timeline travel especially: decide your
  rules early (fixed past? branching? observer-only?) and never break them — inconsistency here
  destroys credibility faster than anywhere else. Prefer using them to *raise the era's core
  question* (what is a self across timelines?) rather than as a plot escape hatch.
- **Arks / underground cities / refuges:** these are *built before* the catastrophe by people who saw
  it coming — which means a fight over *who gets in*. That selection is one of the richest political
  engines you have: lotteries, bloodlines, bribes, stowaways, the ones left outside. The ark's
  internal society (born in transit, never having seen the origin world) becomes its own culture with
  its own geography-naming (see perspective-geography: the ship is "the world," origin Earth a myth).

## The integration test

Before adding any element, ask:
- What does it *break*? (institutions, relationships, prior tech)
- What does it *cost*, and how does it *fail*?
- Who finds it mundane, and who finds it monstrous? (figure/ground)
- What is it *called*, by whom, and why? (provenance)
- What new *question* does it put at stake for this era?

If you can answer those five, the element is load-bearing. If you can't, it's set dressing — cut it
or develop it.
