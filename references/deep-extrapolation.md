# Deep Extrapolation: the "and then what?" engine

The most common failure in speculative worldbuilding is **stopping at the first layer**. Someone
imagines a technology arriving — "we'll have realistic androids" — and then describes... realistic
androids. That's not a future; it's a product announcement. The future is everything that happens
*after* the product announcement: the second marriage to an android, the law that voids it, the
android-rights riot, the underground that smuggles deprecated models, the religion that forms around
a decommissioned unit. Our present-day guess at how a future technology will be used is, almost
certainly, a ten-thousandth of how it will actually be used. This file is the method for chasing the
other 9,999 parts.

The stakes are real: people can't plan for a future they can't picture, and they can only picture
the shallow layer. A world that makes consequences *vivid and specific* does what cosmic sociology
does in Three-Body — it's a rehearsal, a warning, a way of seeing targets others can't yet see.
Treat every speculative element as an obligation to extrapolate honestly and far.

## Tool 1 — The order-of-effects ladder

For any change, force yourself down at least three rungs. Each rung asks "...and then what?" of the
rung above. The interesting worldbuilding lives on rungs 2-4, almost never on rung 1.

1. **First order — the capability.** What can now be done that couldn't before. (The shallow layer.
   Necessary, but don't stop here.)
2. **Second order — behavior.** How real people change what they *do* day to day. Who does it first,
   who's forced to, who refuses. Habits, routines, small humiliations and conveniences.
3. **Third order — norms & institutions.** When enough behavior shifts, what rules, laws, markets,
   professions, etiquette, and taboos form, bend, or die. What new job exists; what job is now shameful.
4. **Fourth order — power & worldview.** Who gains and loses leverage; which power structure is
   obsoleted and takes revenge; what becomes thinkable that was unthinkable, and vice versa.
5. **Fifth order — backlash & adaptation.** The counter-movement, the workaround, the black market,
   the nostalgia, the generation that grows up taking it for granted and rebels against *that*.

Write the chain explicitly. If you can't get past rung 2, you haven't found the real future yet.

## Tool 2 — The stakeholder lattice

A change doesn't hit "society"; it hits *specific actors* who each metabolize it differently.
Run every major element through this cast, asking of each: **what do they want, what do they fear,
how do they exploit / resist / repurpose it, and what do they do that no one intended?**

- **Individuals across strata** — the rich early-adopter, the poor who get the degraded version, the
  middle who go into debt for it, the elderly left behind, the child who never knew the before-time.
- **Big corporations** — how they capture, lock in, meter, and bundle it; the business model that
  quietly reshapes the tech to serve extraction; the incumbent it threatens.
- **Governments & militaries** — regulation, taxation, surveillance, weaponization, the version
  classified and the version banned; what a state does when the tech threatens its monopoly on
  violence, money, or identity.
- **Criminal & black markets** — the first to use it seriously, often years ahead of legitimate use;
  smuggling, fraud, new crimes that have no name yet.
- **Religions & ideologies** — sanctification or damnation; schisms over it; the new heresy.
- **Artists, subcultures, status-seekers** — how it becomes fashion, slang, taste, a way to signal;
  the scene that forms; the cool early use vs. the cringe mass use.
- **The excluded & the refusers** — those who can't get it or won't, and what that refusal comes to
  *mean* socially; the new minority, sainted or despised.

Different actors moving at different speeds, in different directions, is what makes a future feel
*populated* rather than announced. Their collisions are your plot and your factions (loop back to
`factions-from-history.md`: factions are stakeholder coalitions over a concrete change).

## Tool 3 — Repurposing prompts (breaking the possibility boundary)

The inventor's intended use is the least interesting use. Deliberately wrench each element sideways:

- **Intimacy & sex.** Almost every communication/embodiment/AI tech goes here fast; it's a huge,
  under-imagined driver. How does this change love, loneliness, fidelity, grief?
- **Crime & deception.** How is it used to steal, lie, impersonate, coerce, hide?
- **Status & cruelty.** How does it become a way to rank people, exclude, humiliate, flex?
- **Grief & memory.** How do people use it to hold onto the dead, the past, a lost self?
- **Worship & meaning.** What do people make sacred with it? What ritual forms?
- **Boredom & play.** The frivolous, addictive, time-wasting use — often the *biggest* real use.
- **War.** The version optimized purely to kill, and what that arms race does.
- **The mundane and the petty.** Taxes, parking, queues, customer service, divorce, inheritance.
  The future is also boring, and the boring uses are where most lives are actually lived.

## Tool 4 — Boundary-pushing questions

When the speculation feels shallow, run these to crack it open:

- **What becomes free/cheap? What becomes scarce/precious?** (When images became free, attention
  became scarce. Apply the same inversion to your element.)
- **What scarcity is now abundant — and what does the old scarcity's priesthood do when obsolete?**
- **What taboo dissolves, and what new taboo forms to replace it?** (Societies seem to need a roughly
  conserved quantity of taboo; when one falls, watch where the next rises.)
- **What's the second technology this collides with?** Effects multiply, don't add. (Uploading × cheap
  fusion × no inheritance law = a gerontocracy of immortal capital — none of those alone gets you there.)
- **What's the time-lag and generational effect?** The first generation fears it, the second
  normalizes it, the third can't imagine life without it and rebels against the second's values.
- **Push to the extreme, then pull back.** Imagine the element at 1000× saturation to find the shape
  of the pressure, then dial back to the messy, partial, unevenly-distributed reality.
- **Invert the obvious prediction.** Everyone assumes X will cause Y; seriously argue it causes anti-Y.

## Tool 5 — Honesty checks (avoid the clichés and the techno-utopia)

- **No frictionless adoption.** New tech arrives broken, expensive, mistrusted, unevenly. The
  friction *is* the story.
- **Distribution is the plot.** Who gets it first and last is more important than what it does.
- **People are not rational adopters.** They use tools for spite, lust, boredom, status, and love far
  more than for the efficiency the brochure promised.
- **Old things don't vanish; they stratify.** Horses didn't disappear; they became luxury and sport.
  Ask what your obsoleted thing *becomes*.
- **Beware the single-variable future.** Real futures are many changes interacting, plus everything
  that *didn't* change and now looks absurd next to what did.

## How to record extrapolations in the codex

Deep extrapolations are the raw material for everything else. When you run one:
- Promising consequences that haven't "fired" yet → log as entries in `09-undercurrents.md`.
- Stakeholder coalitions that emerge → seeds for `02-factions/`.
- New behaviors that need names → `08-naming-lexicon.md`.
- The tech/phenomenon file itself (`assets/templates/03-tech-phenomenon.md`) has a "what it breaks"
  and "new question" section — fill these from the ladder and lattice, not off the top of your head.

## Worked micro-example: cheap, reliable mind-backup arrives

- **Rung 1 (capability):** you can be restored from a backup after death. *(shallow — stop here and
  you have nothing.)*
- **Rung 2 (behavior):** people stop fearing some deaths and start fearing *lapses* (the gap since last
  backup). "When did you last sync?" becomes a normal intimate question. Extreme-sport tourism booms
  for the freshly-backed-up. The poor, who back up rarely, live with a permanence the rich have escaped.
- **Rung 3 (norms/institutions):** life insurance mutates into "backup escrow"; a profession of
  "continuity notaries"; a taboo against deleting a backup without consent; a new shame in *being a
  restore* ("you're not the original, are you?").
- **Rung 4 (power/worldview):** whoever hosts the backups holds the ultimate leverage — refuse to
  restore you and you're truly dead. States and corporations fight over the backup registry (this is
  literally how the Harbour Authority in *Anchorage* gains power). "Death" stops being the great
  equalizer; mortality becomes a class marker.
- **Rung 5 (backlash):** a "true-death" movement that refuses backup as the only authentic life; a
  black market in *stolen* backups (blackmail by threatening to run a copy of you); kidnapping loses
  its meaning but *backup-theft* replaces it.
- **Stakeholder cuts:** the bereaved use it to keep the dead "alive" and can't grieve (grief prompt);
  cults form around perfectly-preserved founders (worship prompt); militaries field soldiers who don't
  fear death and the horror that unlocks (war prompt); teenagers prank-delete each other's recent
  backups (boredom/cruelty prompt).
- **Boundary question:** death became cheap to undo — so what became scarce? *Finality.* The ability
  to truly end, to mean a goodbye, becomes the new luxury and the new tragedy.

Notice none of that is on rung 1. That's the point.
