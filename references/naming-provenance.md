# Naming with Provenance

This is the single highest-leverage technique for a world that feels real. A name like
"Singularity Crystal / 奇点晶体" announces *an author invented this*. A name like "the Lazarus
ward" (a hospital wing where the first revival patients were kept, after the biblical Lazarus)
announces *people lived through this and named it as they went*.

## The principle: nobody names things from scratch

When humans meet something new, they almost never coin a clean original word. They reach for what's
nearby. Every real term is a fossil of the moment and people that produced it. So before you name
anything, answer: **who named this, when, and what did they reach for?**

## The provenance toolkit — pick a mechanism, don't free-invent

1. **Analogy / metaphor from the familiar.** New thing described via old thing it resembles.
   - *computer "bug"*, *web "browser"*, *desktop*, *cloud*. → A drifting interstellar organism gets
     called a "stardust worm" because it looks like a worm in dust. (And note: to *itself*, the dust
     is the sea — see perspective-geography.)
2. **Eponym (named after a person).** Discoverer, victim, villain, the first famous case.
   - *Salk vaccine, Higgs boson, Molotov cocktail.* → The first stable mind-upload is "a Vance"
     after the patient Ava Vance; later, to "go Vance" becomes slang for uploading.
3. **Acronym worn smooth.** A bureaucratic or technical initialism that becomes a normal word.
   - *laser, radar, scuba, NASA.* → Orbital habitats built by the "Closed Ecological Life-support
     Initiative" are just "the Celis" two generations later; nobody expands it anymore.
4. **Brand → common noun (genericization).** The first mover's trademark becomes the category.
   - *to google, xerox, thermos, hoover.* → All neural implants are "Halos" because Halo Inc. shipped
     first, even decades after that company is gone.
5. **Loanword / mishearing / corruption.** Borrowed across a language and bent by the borrower's
   mouth, often misunderstood.
   - *"alligator" from el lagarto; "kangaroo"* lore. → A human word mangled by an alien tongue, or an
     alien word humans approximate and get slightly wrong (and the wrongness becomes canonical).
6. **Euphemism / propaganda / political naming.** The name is a position. The same thing has a
   regime name and a street name.
   - *"collateral damage," "special operation," "the Troubles."* → State calls them "rehabilitation
     arcologies"; the people inside call them "the drowned cities."
7. **Numbering / cataloging that escapes the lab.** A serial number or catalog ID becomes the name.
   - *Boeing 747, K2, M-theory, COVID-19.* → A defective prosthetic line known forever by its recall
     code, "the 8-series," because that recall is what people remember.
8. **Place- or event-naming after where/when it happened.**
   - *Spanish flu, Marathon, sandwich (after the Earl).* → A treaty is "the Ceres Accord"; a war is
     "the Long Quiet" for the comms blackout that defined it.

## Layering: the same thing has several names

Real important things accrue *multiple* names by register and faction. Give your central concepts at
least two: an official/technical name and a vernacular/loaded one. The gap between them carries
politics and time.

- Official: *"involuntary consciousness suspension."* Street: *"getting shelved."*
- Technical: *"trans-luminal corridor."* Spacer slang: *"the throat."* Ad copy: *"the Skip."*

## Naming conventions belong to cultures

Different peoples name by different logics, and that *is* characterization. Record each culture's
convention in its `05-peoples-and-species/` file:

- A merchant culture names ships after debts and ledgers.
- A devout culture names children after the dead, places after miracles.
- A military bureaucracy names everything by function-code; their poets rebel by naming things after
  weather.
- An AI civilization might name by hash or by precise timestamp; humans shorten these into nicknames,
  which the AIs find by turns charming and insulting.

When a new culture or era arrives, it *renames* inherited things — see perspective-geography.

## Quick test before you commit a name

- Can I state, in one sentence, *who named it and what they reached for*? If no → don't use it.
- Would two different factions plausibly call it the same thing? If yes, maybe it needs a second,
  loaded name.
- Does it sound like a marketing deck ("Quantum Nexus Prime")? If yes → reground it in a person,
  place, accident, or old word.
