# Emergent History: making time feel caused

The failure mode of fictional history is the **decree**: "In 2147 the corporations seized power."
It reads like a textbook because it skips the only interesting part — *how*. The fix is to treat
history as an accumulation of small, traceable pressures that tip into sudden change. This is also
what gives a participant (player/reader) the feeling of being *inside* the current of history.

## The shape: undercurrent → trigger → cascade → new normal

Real upheavals look gradual-then-sudden. Model every major turn in four beats:

1. **Undercurrent (the slow part).** One or more pressures building quietly for years, each
   individually unremarkable, logged in `09-undercurrents.md`. Examples: a widening gap between
   licensed and black-market prosthetic firmware; a generation that grew up never having met an
   un-augmented adult; a colony whose air-recycler patents are held off-world.
2. **Trigger (the small part).** A specific, almost petty event that wouldn't matter except that the
   ground was already saturated. A single recalled batch. One leaked memo. A border guard's mistake.
   The smallness is the point — it shows the system was *primed*, not that fate intervened.
3. **Cascade (the fast part).** The trigger propagates through the pressures, each one releasing the
   next. Write this as a chain you can follow, not a montage.
4. **New normal (the aftermath).** The world on the far side: new vocabulary, redrawn maps, split or
   newborn factions, a *different question* now at stake. The old problem is settled or obsolete.

Write big turns by filling these four beats explicitly, then distributing the consequences across
the codex (timeline, factions, geography, lexicon).

## Decisions accumulate (the "you were there" feeling)

For interactive media especially, make the macro-history legible as the sum of micro-choices:

- **Keep choices small and local.** Players rarely "choose to start a war." They under-report an
  anomaly, side with one engineer over another, divert a shipment. The war is the *integral* of a
  thousand such acts. Design decision points at that granularity.
- **Show the ledger later.** When a leap happens, let the player glimpse how their small acts fed the
  undercurrent ("the firmware you signed off on is the one that failed"). This is what produces awe
  rather than a cutscene.
- **Delay and diffuse consequences.** A choice that pays off three eras later, through an
  intermediary the player never met, feels like history. Immediate karmic payoff feels like a game.

## Eras should be alien to each other

Across a long timeline, don't just raise the tech level — change the *kind* of question:

| Era feel | The question at stake | What's unthinkable here |
|---|---|---|
| Near-future | Who owns my body's data? | That the body is optional |
| Divergence | Is a copied mind the same person? | That minds and matter are separable cheaply |
| Post-human | What do we owe instances we can delete? | That death was once permanent and singular |
| Alien-contact | Whose physics is even correct? | That "we" referred only to humans |

When you advance the timeline, ask: *would a person from the previous era even understand the
argument people are now having?* If yes, you haven't moved far enough.

## Techniques for texture

- **Date things unevenly.** Real history clusters — quiet decades, then five frantic years. Don't
  spread events at regular intervals.
- **Let causes be mixed and partly wrong.** People at the time misattribute causes; later historians
  disagree. Encode this — a timeline entry can carry a "disputed" note. It implies a real past.
- **Preserve residue.** Old institutions outlive their purpose (a "Ministry of Orbital Mail" that now
  runs the secret police). Names lag function — that lag is history made visible.
- **Plant before you pay off.** Anything that erupts should have been visible, small, earlier. Seed
  it in `09-undercurrents.md` even if you don't know yet how it detonates.

## Checklist for any major historical turn

- [ ] Is the trigger small and the pressure large? (Not the reverse.)
- [ ] Can the reader trace macro → micro causes?
- [ ] Did the *question at stake* change, not just the scenery?
- [ ] Did I redistribute consequences: lexicon, maps, factions, characters — not just the timeline?
- [ ] Did I plant at least one new undercurrent for the *next* leap?
- [ ] CHANGELOG updated?
