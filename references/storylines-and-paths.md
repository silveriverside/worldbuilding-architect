# Storylines and Development Paths

A world is interesting because many futures are still live in it. This file covers two related
crafts: **weaving plotlines** (main/hidden/side, intersecting) and **keeping the world open** to
multiple development directions — the grand-strategy quality of "scripted history that still feels
free."

## Part 1 — The web of storylines

Don't think of plot as a single line. Think of a *web* recorded in `07-storylines/_index.md`, where
each strand has a type and the strands cross at shared events, places, and characters.

### Types of strand

- **Mainline (open).** The visible spine the audience knows it's following — a war, a voyage, a
  succession. Stated goals, public stakes.
- **Mainline (hidden).** A second spine of equal weight running *underneath*, usually about who's
  really causing the visible events. Revealed in turns. The hidden line recontextualizes the open
  one (the comfort of Three-Body, Dune's prescience plots, noir conspiracies). The art is that
  clues to the hidden line are seeded in plain sight along the open one.
- **Side-arcs.** Smaller, local stories — a character's debt, a town's feud, a faction's schism.
  They have their own beginnings and ends but should *touch* the mainlines: a side-arc's resolution
  becomes a mainline's trigger, or a mainline's upheaval is the backdrop that forces a side-arc.

### Making lines cross (the part that makes it feel woven)

Lines feel connected when they share **anchors**: a place both pass through, a character who belongs
to both, an object that travels between them, or a single event seen from two lines' angles. When you
add a strand, deliberately attach it to ≥2 existing anchors. Record crossings explicitly in the
index so consistency holds.

Good crossing patterns:
- **The unwitting hinge.** A side-character's small choice (open line) is the trigger the hidden line
  was waiting for. Neither side knows.
- **The same event, two meanings.** A festival to the open line is a cover for an exchange to the
  hidden line. Write it once; annotate both readings.
- **Delayed convergence.** Two lines run parallel for a long time, sharing only a recurring motif,
  then collide at a turn. Plant the motif early.

### Open vs. hidden: managing the reveal

- The hidden line should be *fully real from the start* — you (the author) know it; it's causing
  things — even though it's revealed late. Don't invent it at the reveal; that reads as a cheat.
- Leave a trail: each hidden beat should leave a visible fingerprint on the open line that, in
  hindsight, only fits the hidden explanation.
- A reveal should *reframe*, not just *inform*: after it, earlier scenes should mean something new.

## Part 2 — Multiple development directions (not win conditions)

The user's key point: the world should support genuinely different *directions of development*, not a
win/lose binary — though several distinct "victories" and many non-victories can coexist. This is the
Stellaris / grand-strategy feeling: history is on rails enough to have momentum and flavor, yet open
enough that you author your own path.

### Branch points, not endings

Design the world as a tree of **branch points** — moments where a concrete choice (by a player, or
by the narrative) sends the world toward a different *kind* of future. Each branch should:
- Flow from the factions' real disagreements (see `factions-from-history.md`) — branches are
  factions' incompatible futures made selectable.
- Lead to a *different world*, not a different score. (Uploading civilization vs. embodiment
  civilization vs. a brokered hybrid vs. collapse-and-reset — four worlds, none simply "winning.")
- Stay reachable from multiple starting positions, so the path feels chosen, not assigned.

### How grand-strategy keeps "history" and "freedom" at once

Lessons to apply (Stellaris, CK, Paradox-style; also Three-Body's faction logic):

1. **Scripted spine + emergent flesh.** A backbone of likely events gives flavor and momentum (the
   "history"); systemic interactions and player choice fill in the specifics (the "freedom"). Author
   the spine in the timeline; leave the flesh to play out.
2. **Pressures, not scripts, drive events.** Tie events to *conditions* (an undercurrent crossing a
   threshold) rather than fixed dates. Then the same event can fire early, late, or never — history
   rhymes without repeating. (This is the mechanical twin of `emergent-history.md`.)
3. **Crises as great filters.** Periodic world-level crises (a contact, a rebellion of minds, an
   ecological tip) force every faction to take a position, reshuffling the board and opening new
   branches. These are your qualitative leaps, made interactive.
4. **No single victory axis.** Offer several incommensurable goals (survival, transcendence,
   contact, restoration, dominance) so different players pursue different ends and the world has no
   "correct" direction.
5. **Path-dependence & residue.** Earlier choices constrain and flavor later ones; dead branches
   leave ruins, refugees, and grudges in the world even if not taken. The world remembers.

### Recording it

In `07-storylines/_index.md`, keep a **branch map**: the major branch points, the condition/choice
that selects each, the *kind of world* each leads to, and which factions/undercurrents feed it.
Branches should link back to the undercurrents that make them possible, so the open futures are
grounded in the world's real tensions, not bolted on.

## Checklist

- [ ] Does the hidden mainline already exist and leave fingerprints on the open one?
- [ ] Does each new strand attach to ≥2 existing anchors (place/character/object/event)?
- [ ] Do branch points lead to *different worlds*, not different scores?
- [ ] Are branches rooted in faction disagreements and logged undercurrents?
- [ ] Are events tied to conditions/pressures rather than fixed fate?
- [ ] Do untaken paths still leave residue in the world?
